01 / The PitchLand War to Void War

Same meritocracy, same hidden strings, a whole new battlefield.

In the canon, a Gold who survives the Institute goes on to the Academy: a school in the Asteroid Belt where the young elite learn to command starfleets. It is the natural, canonical sequel to your first game. The Institute taught you to take a castle; the Academy teaches you to take a ship. And it hands you, ready-made, the single sharpest theme in Red Rising: you can do everything right and still be cheated of victory by the powerful. Darrow does not win the Academy. That defeat, and the betrayal that follows it, launches the war that consumes the rest of the series.

The design opportunity is unusually clean because the engine already exists in The Institute. Buried in the current build is a complete fleet-combat simulation (fbSim, fbStartSim, ship types with hull, crew, gunnery, boarding and routing) that today is only ever auto-resolved inside the narrative campaign. The Academy is, in large part, the game you get when you pull that latent system into the light and build a campaign around it.

02 / Canon vs InventedWhat the Books Give Us, and What We Build

The Academy is a real place in the novels but a thinly drawn one: it is essentially the prologue of Golden Son, not a fully mapped institution like the Institute. Everything below is tagged Canon (safe to build on) or Invented (our extrapolation, to be designed rather than quoted as lore).

03 / The Core LoopThe Asteroid War

The Academy's campaign is a mirror of the Institute's, translated into three dimensions. Where the Institute had a valley of castles, the Academy has a field of asteroids. Where you marched columns, you jump fleets.

1 · Muster at the Can.

Your patron's funding buys and crews your starting fleet: a mix of light hunters, boarding craft and a capital flagship. Better patrons, and better standing, buy bigger fleets. This is the Academy's version of recruiting at your castle.

2 · Jump the field.

The strategic map is the asteroid belt. Fleets jump rock to rock, spending a movement/fuel budget. Asteroids hold supply bases (fuel, munitions, repair, crew) that you seize and hold. Fog of the void hides rival fleets until scouts or contact reveal them. This is march, zone of control and fog, reused.

3 · Contact becomes battle.

Move a fleet into a rival and the void battle fires there and then, exactly like the Institute's move-triggered combat. Win with jumps to spare and hunt on; lose and limp back crippled. Ships are precious, so the doctrine is to cripple and board, not destroy (see below).

4 · Last fleet standing.

The mock war ends when one cadet's fleet holds the field, and the winner is raised to Praetor. But the Proctors watch, and the Sovereign has favourites. Doing everything right may not be enough.

Why this reuses so much. March, ETA, zone of control, fog with memory, move-triggered battles, occupy-to-hold, supply and upkeep, five-route victory, coalition diplomacy: every one of these systems already exists and works in The Institute. The Academy re-skins the strategic layer from valley to void and swaps the battle layer from the land sim to the fleet sim you already own.

04 / Void WarfareThe Signature: Board, Don't Break

The one thing that makes Red Rising space combat unlike any other is that warships are so expensive you fight to capture them intact. A destroyer costs tens of billions; a dreadnought is a floating continent. So battles are not a gunnery duel to the death, they are a dance of maneuver, shielding and marine boarding. This should drive the entire battle layer.

The battle beats

  • Gunnery phase. Railguns and missiles trade fire at range. Pulseshields soak damage and overheat; sustained fire drops a shield and lets kinetic rounds bite the hull. This is a shield gauge and a hull gauge, already modelled in fbSim.
  • Close and cripple. You do not want a kill, you want a mission-kill: knock out engines and shields so a ship is dead in the void and ripe to board.
  • The boarding action. LeechCraft latch onto a crippled hull, burn through with a thermal drill behind a shield, and pour in marines (Obsidians, Grays, a Gold champion). A crippled ship is then won in the corridors, crew against crew, under failing gravity. Capture it and it joins your fleet for the rest of the campaign.
  • Rout and strike craft. RipWing fighters screen the capitals and hunt boarding craft; a broken crew routs, exactly as a broken squad routs on land.
The hook: every battle is a risk calculus. Destroy the enemy flagship and you win the fight but gain nothing. Cripple and board it and you win the fight and add a capital ship to your fleet, at the risk of a bloody, uncertain marine assault. That single tension is a whole game's worth of decisions, and it is pure Red Rising.

The Iron Rain (the set-piece)

The signature spectacle of the setting: Golds and Obsidians in starShell armour fired from orbit down onto a planet or moon through spitTubes, Grays dropping in hivepods. As a game mode, the Iron Rain is the Academy's siege: once you hold the void above an objective, you launch an orbital assault to take the surface, blending your fleet layer with a ground assault the Institute's battle engine can render. Rare, costly, unforgettable.

06 / Progression & PoliticsPatron, Rank, and the Strings

The Institute's progression was standards and Primus bars. The Academy's is a career, and it is openly rigged, which is the point.

  • Patron. You do not enter the Academy on merit alone; a Gold House sponsors you. Your patron sets your starting fleet and expects returns. Disappoint them, as Darrow disappoints Nero au Augustus, and they can sever you mid-campaign, stripping your protection and rank. A living relationship gauge, not a menu.
  • Rank ladder. Cadet to Lancer to Praetor to Imperator. Invented for the game (canon has no Academy merit bars), but grounded in the real naval ranks the books use.
  • Favour and Allegiance. The narrative campaign in The Institute already tracks a patron's favour and your allegiance; the Academy makes these first-class. Favour buys ships and protection; allegiance is who you truly serve, and it can curdle.
  • The rigged victory. The defining mechanic. The Proctors and the Sovereign can and do interfere: a rival slips your lines, a "malfunction" costs you a flagship, a judgement goes against you. The player should learn that a clean tactical win is necessary but not sufficient, and that surviving the strings, or cutting them, is the real game. This turns Darrow's canonical humiliation into your central drama.

07 / ContinuityCarrying The Institute Forward

The two games are a matched pair, and the sequel should feel like it remembers the first.

Bring your house.

Let a completed Institute run seed your Academy start: the house you took a standard for, the standing you built, the honour or menace you earned, even a saved character from the creator. A prestigious Institute record buys a better patron.

Bring your people.

Darrow commands his Academy fleet with his old Institute lieutenants, Roque and Tactus. Officers you forged in the valley return as ship captains in the void. Rivals return too: beat House Bellona at the Institute and Karnus is waiting for you at the Academy.

Mirror the beats.

Same meritocracy, same hidden strings, same betrayals, escalated. The Institute ends in triumph; the Academy, faithful to canon, can end in a defeat that starts a war, setting up a third act.

08 / VictoryMore Than One Way to Win the Void

The Institute ships five victory routes; the Academy should inherit that breadth, re-themed.

RouteHow you win
ConquestBe the last fleet in the field. The straight military win.
CaptureWin by taking, not sinking: reach a threshold of boarded, captured enemy hulls. The doctrine victory.
CommandPrestige: graduate top of the class with your fleet and crews intact, made Praetor cleanly.
PatronagePolitical: bind enough Houses and win the Sovereign's favour, whatever the scoreboard says.
RisingThe hidden route: if the game carries the series' rebellion, turn your fleet against the Society itself. The Red Rising ending.

09 / Tone & LookAureate Gold on Void Black

The Institute's palette was gold, marble and blood. The Academy keeps the aureate gold and adds the cold of space: void black, asteroid grey, the sterile fluorescents of the Can against the warm gold of the officer class. Roman and mythological naming continues (ships and Houses), with a Norse layer for the Obsidian crews. The rose petals, red for Gold, white for the rest, are the emotional signature. Hold the series' refusal of clean power fantasy: ships full of people you are responsible for, wins that cost, and the constant corrosion of wearing the oppressor's uniform.

10 / Build PathFrom Latent Engine to Sequel

Because the fleet sim already exists, the Academy can be reached in stages rather than built from nothing.

Stage 1 · surface the engine · days
Play a single void battle
  • Wire fbStartSim into an interactive battle screen (the sim already runs; it just needs a camera, controls and a render pass). This is the Institute's Horizon 1 item and the Academy's foundation stone at once.
  • Add the shield and hull gauges and a first boarding action to the UI.
Stage 2 · the asteroid campaign · weeks
A strategic layer in the void
  • Re-skin the campaign map: asteroids for lands, jumps for marches, supply bases for works, fleets for armies. Reuse march/ZoC/fog/occupy wholesale.
  • Fleet roster and the capture-not-kill loop; patron and rank progression; the rigged-victory system.
Stage 3 · the full sequel · months
Iron Rain, continuity, the Rising
  • Iron Rain as the void-to-ground set-piece, bridging fleet and land battle engines.
  • Institute-to-Academy save carry (house, standing, officers, rivals).
  • The five void victory routes, the branching narrative campaign, and the defeat-that-starts-a-war ending.
The strategic case: a sequel usually means a second engine. Here it does not. The Academy is mostly a re-theme of systems The Institute already ships plus the fleet sim it already contains. That is the cheapest sequel you will ever get to build, and the most faithful to the source.
